When People Rise: Same-Sex Marriage in Progress in Taiwan

Although same-sex marriage has been widely discussed by the society in Taiwan, the country is still facing major challenges to pass the law due to backlashes of the conservative communities.

By Jack Liu

After the U.S. legalized same-sex marriage, it has become an international issue widely discussed around the global. Many wonder, after the major shift of the continent, which country will be the next. Among the countries being discussed, Taiwan, a sovereign island located in Eastern Asia, brings about a silver lining. Taiwan is considered to have the most active gay society in Asia. The most magnificent gay parade in Asia is also held in Taiwan. Each year, it attracts over 80,000 people globally.

Although there is an active gay community in Taiwan, there are still quarrels between the pro-same-sex marriage community and conservative community.

Concerning that the traditional family value (a one male, one female family) will collapse as well as the uncertain consequences after enacting same-sex marriage law, those conservative communities has as well involved themselves in this battle of legalizing same-sex marriage. As the arguments continue within both communities, it leads to a divided society.

Nonetheless, certain urgent issues have raised during the dispute of two communities, one of which is the medical right of same-sex couples. In the past, same-sex couples are subjected to law that they are not allowed to sign consentaneous documents of surgery for their partner. However, many of them have lack connection or have already cut of their relationships with their family. It leads to a dilemmatic situation for the doctors while deciding whether or not to perform a surgery.

Recognizing this situation, several city/county governments in Taiwan launched the same-sex couple registration. The registration does not have legitimate effect, yet some medical facilities recognized it as a proof of “family member”, allowing those partners to sign the documents for their love one. It is considered as a temporary method to solve such problem. Some pro-same-sex marriage communities also regarded as a milestone of the marriage equality movement.

Views from the government on same-sex marriage

In Taiwan, citizens look up to justices and the Legislative Yuan (parliament) to determine whether or not to legalize same-sex marriage. Amid the Legislative Yuan, legislators are reviewing two types of bill. One is called the “Marriage Equality Bill”; the other is called the ”Same-sex Partner Bill”.

Several differences among these two bills are, for instance, how the partner is called legitimately as well as whether or not it is written in the Civil Code. The pro-same-sex marriage communities are in favor of the Marriage Equality Bill, since they think that excluding some groups of the Civil Code is an action of discrimination. They are also confused that if the government is willing to composed a bill which has the exact same right akin to the one amends in the Civil Code, why bothers to draw up a new one? Some skeptics think it is due to compromises of the government to the conservative communities.

A closer look of the issue of AIDS

Amid the dispute, one of the major issues that rises to the surface is the infection of AIDS. AIDS has been one of the sexually transmitted disease negatively imposed on gay people. The conservative communities argue that there have already been many gay people infected with AIDS. They are afraid that once the law is enacted, statistics will increase.

However, there are no solid facts that support this assumption. Chang-hsun Chen, researcher of the Center of Disease Control, Taiwan, said that gay people are more alert to the prevention of AIDS, which they will take sexually transmitted disease test regularly, whereas many heterosexuals do not have a regular testing habit.

Meanwhile, other assumptions were introduced. For instance, some pro-same-sex marriage communities think that perhaps this is the reason why the statistic rocketed. However, Yang-Tai Lin, a member of Federation of Medical Students in Taiwan, said that there are also no actual proofs to support this surmise.

The data released by the Center of Disease Control, Taiwan can give us a closer look on how AIDS has transmitted to others. The data illustrates how people get infected by certain channels, instead of categorizing them as gay people or straight people. It is difficult to analyze how many gay or straight people are infected annually, since some people may have same-sex intercourse yet denying that they are gay people, vice versa.
